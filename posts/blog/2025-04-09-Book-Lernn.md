I read in a book, that Socrates said not to trust what you learn only from books. On a trip to Brazil, ([problematic](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=TwKpj2ISQAc)) physicist [Richard Feynman noted that students at one university](https://v.cx/2010/04/feynman-brazil-education) had memorized portions of text, but had little to no practical experience with the ideas presented, or experimental challenges to puzzle through.

[Socrates's point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phaedrus_(dialogue)#Discussion_of_rhetoric_and_writing_(257c–279c)), as I understand it, was that it's easy to learn the wrong thing, or to have a completely useless mental model of how a described thing behaves or what properties it has. Socrates argues that having a mentor is vital in the education practice, someone who has hands on experience with the material, and can provide a response to hypothetical challenges that a student can inquire about.

Merlin Mann, on the podcast ["Roderick on the Line"](https://www.merlinmann.com/rotl-episodes/), discussed in an episode that someone could build false confidence in their craftsmanship skills from their extensive history of watching woodworking videos. The folly implied is that skill, especially in craft, is built through "[doing the hard thing](https://www.43folders.com/2011/04/22/cranking)" and actually [getting to work](https://www.backtowork.limo/).

I've heard people argue around the 1980's about eventually having a "digital Socrates" that can grow and evolve with each student. It seems that [Steve Wozniak is skeptical of LLM](https://www.wxii12.com/article/north-carolina-apple-co-founder-steve-wozniak-artificial-intelligence-hpu-students/46870828)'s attempt to act in that capacity.

> “We aren’t skeptical enough,” Wozniak said. “And especially with generative AI and ChatGPT, you can get a whole beautiful document written and it’s just so cool, but it still doesn’t have that emotional content like it came from a human thinking, a student thinking.”

I'll set aside the problem that no one reads (like sending a customer an email reply with the simple fix to an urgent issue) for another time.

So the reason I'm writing this now, is that Linked-In Learning (formerly Linda.com) now integrates into the course player UI an LLM chat window fed with a prompt that includes (I assume) the transcript of whatever course you're watching. And what breaks my brain, given the above lesson from Greece, is that making a robot that's read a text (essentially a book), and then presenting it as though it's an expert at that material, is incredibly damning.

Material fed into an LLM has no context for the text in any sort of material, real-world existence. There are levels of nuance and implications that a piece of writing is not to be taken as an account of actual events. As such, [Google's LLM was tricked](https://www.morningbrew.com/stories/2024/05/24/google-s-ai-says-you-should-eat-pebbles) into believing [an Onion article](https://theonion.com/geologists-recommend-eating-at-least-one-small-rock-per-1846655112/) where a fictional geologist was said to recommend eating 1-2 small rocks a day for health. Being that LLMs are not generally part of our world, and [don't pilot bodies of meat](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=T6JFTmQCFHg) with our [specific](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ZE_OlyBhr1A) [biochemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grape_toxicity_in_dogs), they are like the memorizing Brazilian science students, gathering and juggling written knowledge with no practical experience risking it on fragile bodies such as ours.

Particularly, I want to suggest this thought experiment, that if you were to take a fictional account of some story, and present it to the LLM as the fact of the course, whether that's in the initial prompt, or as part of the training set, how and when will the LLM behave such that it "realizes" that this fictional story is just that, and doesn't align with the outside world? This is a key point in personal development, that we actually learn from mistakes, where LLMs can't by design as a prompt exists as a temporary instance of the "mind" and fighting misinformation or defeating a logical fallacy in one will likely just result in the lesson being "forgotten" in the next instance.

Like when I worked at GameStop, and as the people-pleaser that I wanted to be, it killed me when I'd have to refuse the dreams of children wanting to afford their next fix by explaining that wrapping your controller cords tightly leads to the cables pulling out and we would not be buying them, even for the fraction of value we were willing to exchange in store credit. No, if someone came in with a PlayStation 2, caked in dust, cat hair, and cigarette smoke, there was no warning strong enough that I could give this person in front of me that could reverse the damage to the unit on my counter in front of me, nor would it prevent the next customer from coming in with a similar shame.

And similar to how people can change for the better and the worse, I assume such a persistent, teachable AI could become just as malleable as a naive child. Lacking any way to challenge it's own precepts, algorithms as a means to replace human skepticism when human risks are present are fundamentally untrustworthy to our ends.
